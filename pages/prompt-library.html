Needs Analysis 7 prompts
01 · NEEDS ANALYSIS
SME Interview Question Generator
Use when: preparing for your first SME discovery call
You are an experienced instructional designer conducting a needs analysis for a new course. Generate 12 discovery questions for an SME interview about [TOPIC, e.g. "workplace safety for construction workers"]. The audience is [LEARNER ROLE, e.g. "frontline supervisors, 5-15 yrs experience"]. The business context is [CONTEXT, e.g. "3 incidents in the last 6 months, leadership wants mandatory training"]. Structure the questions across: current performance gaps, root causes, existing knowledge, barriers to change, and success metrics. Include one question that surfaces what the SME thinks learners already know but actually don't.
Filled exampleTopic: "Customer complaint handling" · Audience: "Call centre agents, 6-18 months experience" · Context: "CSAT scores dropped 12% after a process change"
02 · NEEDS ANALYSIS
Performance Gap Diagnosis
Use when: a stakeholder says "we need training" but you're not sure it's a training problem
I'm an instructional designer reviewing a potential training request. Help me diagnose whether this is a training problem or a performance system problem. The situation: [DESCRIBE THE SITUATION, e.g. "Sales reps are missing their quarterly targets. Manager says they need product knowledge training."] Ask me 5 diagnostic questions — one at a time — to determine whether the gap is caused by: lack of knowledge/skill, lack of motivation, unclear expectations, lack of tools/resources, or consequences not aligned with desired behaviour. After my answers, give me a verdict with your reasoning and recommend the appropriate intervention type.
Filled exampleSituation: "New hires are making errors in the onboarding process. HR wants a 4-hour compliance e-learning module."
03 · NEEDS ANALYSIS
Learner Persona Builder

For each persona include: - Name, role, and a one-line backstory - What they already know about this topic - What they think they know (but don't) - Their biggest frustration related to this topic - How they prefer to learn (and what they resist) - What success looks like for them personally - One quote they might say in a training session Make them feel real, not like HR job descriptions.
Filled exampleTopic: "Data privacy and GDPR" · Audience: "Marketing team members, mix of digital natives and people who joined before social media existed"

06 · NEEDS ANALYSIS
Task Analysis Breakdown
Use when: you need to deconstruct a complex skill or procedure into teachable steps
Perform a cognitive task analysis for the following skill or procedure: Skill: [SKILL OR TASK, e.g. "Conducting a performance review conversation"] Performer level: [NOVICE / INTERMEDIATE / EXPERT] Context: [WHERE THIS HAPPENS, e.g. "1:1 meeting between manager and direct report"] Break it into: 1. Pre-conditions (what the performer needs to know/have before starting) 2. Step-by-step procedure (what they physically/cognitively do) 3. 07 · NEEDS ANALYSIS
Action Mapping Starter
Use when: beginning a Cathy Moore-style action map for a performance problem
Help me build an action map for a training project. Business goal: [BUSINESS GOAL, e.g. "Reduce customer churn rate by 15% in 6 months"] The people who need to change behaviour: [AUDIENCE, e.g. "Customer success managers"] Step 1: Identify 4-6 specific actions these people need to take differently to achieve the business goal. Not "understand" or "know" — observable actions. Step 2: For each action, identify what's currently stopping them (knowledge gap, motivation, environment, tools). Step 3: For each knowledge gap, suggest the minimum content needed — not everything they could know, just what they need to do the action. Output as a structured list, not a paragraph.
Filled exampleGoal: "Increase upsell revenue by 20%" · Audience: "Retail store associates in a telecom company"

Learning Objectives 6 prompts
08 · OBJECTIVES
Objectives at the Right Bloom's Level
Use when: writing objectives and unsure which cognitive level is right
Write 5 learning objectives for a course on [TOPIC]. The learners are [AUDIENCE]. They will use this knowledge/skill in [CONTEXT WHERE THEY'LL APPLY IT]. The desired Bloom's taxonomy level is [LEVEL: Remember / Understand / Apply / Analyse / Evaluate / Create]. Rules: - Use strong, measurable action verbs (no "understand", "know", or "appreciate") - Each objective should be testable — I should be able to design an assessment for it - Format: "By the end of this module, learners will be able to [VERB] [OBJECT] [CONDITION/STANDARD]" - Flag if any objective I could combine or if I'm missing a critical one
Filled exampleTopic: "Conflict resolution for team leads" · Audience: "Newly promoted managers" · Context: "Weekly team meetings and 1:1s" · Level: Apply
09 · OBJECTIVES
Objective Audit — Fix My Draft
Use when: you've written objectives and want to stress-test them
Audit these learning objectives and improve them: [PASTE YOUR CURRENT OBJECTIVES] For each one: 1. Identify the Bloom's level (as written) 2. Flag if the verb is weak or untestable 3. Flag if the objective describes activities rather than outcomes 4. Rewrite it at the appropriate Bloom's level for the course 5. Suggest a brief assessment activity that would test this objective Be direct. If an objective is fundamentally broken, say so and explain why.
Filled examplePaste objectives like: "Learners will understand the importance of cybersecurity" / "Participants will be aware of data protection rules"

12 · OBJECTIVES
Attitude / Behaviour Objective Writer
Use when: the training goal involves changing mindsets, not just skills
Write learning objectives for a training that needs to change attitude or behaviour, not just build knowledge. The behavioural change needed: [DESCRIBE THE CHANGE, e.g. "Managers need to move from task-assigning to coaching conversations"] Current state: [WHAT THEY DO NOW] Desired state: [WHAT THEY SHOULD DO] Audience: [AUDIENCE] Important: attitude change can't be directly measured. 13 · OBJECTIVES
Terminal vs Enabling Objectives
Use when: designing a multi-step course that builds toward a final performance
For this terminal objective, write 4-6 enabling objectives that scaffold toward it. Terminal objective: [YOUR TERMINAL OBJECTIVE, e.g. "Given a client brief, learners will be able to design a compliant, needs-mapped L&D programme within 5 working days"] Audience: [AUDIENCE, e.g. "Junior L&D business partners, 0-2 years in role"] Enabling objectives should: - Build prerequisite knowledge and skills in logical order - Connect explicitly to the terminal objective - Cover knowledge, process, and judgment — not just facts - Be teachable in distinct modules Flag any enabling objective that I could skip if learners already have the prerequisite skill.
Filled exampleTerminal: "Learners will be able to identify and respond to signs of financial vulnerability in a customer conversation in real time"

Scenario Writing 7 prompts
14 · SCENARIOS
Branching Scenario Builder
Use when: designing a decision-based scenario with meaningful consequences
Write a branching scenario for an e-learning course on [TOPIC]. Learner role: [WHO THEY PLAY IN THE SCENARIO, e.g. "a team lead"] The decision they face: [CORE DECISION, e.g. "whether to report a colleague's safety violation"] Stakes: [WHAT'S AT RISK, e.g. "team relationships, personal reputation, legal liability"] Structure: - Opening situation (2-3 sentences, realistic, immediate) - 3 choices (one clearly wrong, one appealing-but-wrong, one right-but-difficult) - Consequence for each choice (realistic, not punitive — show real-world impact) - A follow-up decision for the correct branch only Make the "wrong but tempting" choice genuinely tempting — the one a real person would actually pick under pressure.
Filled exampleTopic: "Giving feedback to a defensive colleague" · Role: "Project manager" · Decision: "How to address missed deadlines without damaging the relationship"
15 · SCENARIOS
Realistic Character Creator
Use when: you need believable characters for scenario-based learning
Create 3 realistic characters for scenarios in a course about [TOPIC]. The course audience is [AUDIENCE]. The characters will appear as colleagues, customers, or managers that learners interact with. For each character: - Name and role (specific, not generic) - Communication style and personality (one telling detail, not a list) - Their default reaction to the situation: what they do/say before the learner intervenes - What they actually need (vs what they appear to want) - One piece of dialogue that immediately establishes their character Avoid stereotypes. Make at least one character surprising — not what you'd expect from their role.
Filled exampleTopic: "Handling difficult conversations about performance" · Audience: "People managers in a tech company"
16 · SCENARIOS

Each opener should: - Drop the learner in the middle of a situation (in medias res — no preamble) - Create immediate stakes or tension - Be under 40 words - Avoid starting with "You are a..." or "Imagine you are..." - Use present tense The audience is [AUDIENCE]. The performance gap this scenario addresses is [PERFORMANCE GAP]. Label each by the emotional hook it uses (urgency, curiosity, discomfort, empathy, consequence).
Filled exampleTopic: "Spotting early signs of employee disengagement" · Audience: "Line managers" · Gap: "Managers wait too long before having check-in conversations"
17 · SCENARIOS
Wrong-But-Tempting Distractor Generator
Use when: your scenario choices are too obvious and learners are guessing correctly without thinking
I have a scenario with a correct answer. Help me write 2 distractors that are wrong but genuinely tempting. The scenario: [DESCRIBE THE SITUATION] The correct choice: [WHAT THE LEARNER SHOULD DO] The learning objective this tests: [OBJECTIVE] The audience: [AUDIENCE] Distractor 1: The choice that feels efficient or "common sense" but misses the nuance Distractor 2: The choice that follows the rules but for the wrong reason, or at the wrong time For each distractor: - Write the choice text (15-25 words) - Write the consequence/feedback (what actually happens, and why this was wrong) - Explain the cognitive trap that makes this choice appealing
Filled exampleScenario: "A customer is clearly upset. They've been on hold for 20 minutes." · Correct: "Acknowledge the wait before anything else"
18 · SCENARIOS
Consequence Feedback Writer
Use when: writing the feedback text after each scenario choice
Write consequence feedback for each of these scenario choices. The feedback should show real-world impact — not just "correct/incorrect." Topic: [TOPIC] Scenario summary: [BRIEF SITUATION DESCRIPTION] Choice A (correct): [CHOICE TEXT] Choice B (wrong — avoidance): [CHOICE TEXT] Choice C (wrong — overreaction): [CHOICE TEXT] For each feedback block: - Show what happens next (the real consequence, 2-3 sentences) - Explain the principle behind the correct/incorrect approach - For wrong choices: don't be punitive — show the human cost, not just the rule broken - For the correct choice: don't over-celebrate — acknowledge the difficulty - Max 80 words per feedback block
Filled exampleTopic: "Raising a compliance concern with your manager" · A (correct): "I schedule a private conversation and bring documentation" · B: "I say nothing and hope someone else reports it" · C: "I escalate directly to the compliance team without talking to my manager"
19 · SCENARIOS
Mini-Case Study Generator
Use when: you need a short real-world case to anchor a module
Write a mini case study (250-350 words) for a module on [TOPIC]. The case should: - Feature a realistic workplace situation (not a textbook example) - Introduce a character with a specific problem or decision - Build tension without resolving it (the learner will decide the outcome) - Include one detail that reveals what the character got wrong without stating it explicitly - End with a question that prompts reflection, not a quiz Audience: [AUDIENCE] The concept this case illustrates: [KEY CONCEPT] Setting: [INDUSTRY/WORKPLACE TYPE] Do not name the character after a common training character name (no Alex, Sam, or Jordan).
Filled exampleTopic: "Unconscious bias in hiring decisions" · Audience: "Hiring managers" · Concept: "Affinity bias" · Setting: "Fast-growing startup"
20 · SCENARIOS
Simulation Dialogue Writer
Use when: building a conversation-based simulation (like a devlin.ai or custom chatbot)
Write a realistic dialogue for a conversation simulation on [TOPIC]. The learner plays: [LEARNER ROLE] The AI character plays: [CHARACTER NAME AND ROLE] The situation: [SITUATION DESCRIPTION] The skill being practised: [SKILL] Target length: [NUMBER] exchanges Rules: - The AI character should not make it easy. They should be realistic — defensive, distracted, or emotionally charged as appropriate. - Each AI response should present a new micro-challenge for the learner to navigate. - Include one moment where the character's tone shifts based on the learner's approach. - Write the character's internal state in [brackets] before each line — this is for the designer, not the learner. - End on an unresolved note — real conversations rarely wrap up cleanly.
